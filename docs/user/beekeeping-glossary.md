# Beekeeping glossary

ApiaryLens uses ordinary beekeeping language and keeps regional variation visible.
This glossary explains the words used in records and forms; it is not a substitute
for local training, an experienced mentor, or veterinary and regulatory guidance.

## Places, colonies, and equipment

**Apiary** — A place where one or more managed bee colonies are kept. Also called a
bee yard.

**Hive** — The physical home and equipment occupied by a colony. ApiaryLens keeps a
hive's identity and history even when boxes or other components change.

**Colony** — The living bee community: queen, workers, drones when present, brood,
and food stores. A colony occupies a hive.

**Brood box / deep** — A box primarily used for the brood nest. “Deep” describes a
common box size; local equipment and management practices vary.

**Super** — A box generally placed above the brood area for surplus honey storage.
Common sizes include shallow and medium.

**Queen excluder** — A grid placed between boxes that allows workers through while
usually restricting the queen. It is commonly used to keep brood out of honey supers.

**Frame** — A removable structure that supports comb inside a hive box.

**Foundation** — A sheet or guide in a frame on which bees draw comb. Frames can also
be foundationless.

**Comb** — The beeswax cells used for brood, honey, pollen, and space within the nest.

**Bottom board** — The floor of a movable-frame hive. It may be solid or screened.

**Entrance reducer** — A piece that narrows the hive entrance for defense, weather,
or management.

**Inner cover / outer cover** — The covers above the uppermost box. The outer cover
protects the hive from weather.

**Nuc / nucleus colony** — A small established colony, usually supplied on several
frames with a queen, brood, workers, and stores.

**Package** — A quantity of adult bees, usually sold with a caged queen and without
established brood comb.

## Bees and queen history

**Queen** — The colony's primary reproductive female. A colony may replace her
through supersedure, emergency queen rearing, or swarming.

**Worker** — A female bee that performs most colony work and normally does not mate.

**Drone** — A male honey bee whose primary biological role is mating with a queen.

**Queenright** — A colony believed to have a functioning queen. The queen herself
does not have to be seen if other evidence is sufficient.

**Queenless** — A colony believed to lack a functioning queen.

**Marked queen** — A queen carrying a paint mark or numbered disc/tag. The
international year-color cycle is white for years ending 1/6, yellow 2/7, red 3/8,
green 4/9, and blue 5/0.

**Supersedure** — Colony replacement of a queen, often while the existing queen is
still present. This differs from replacing a queen as part of colony reproduction by
swarming.

**Swarm** — A queen and a large group of workers leaving to establish a new colony.
“Swarm” can also refer to that group after it leaves.

**Absconding** — Most or all adult bees abandon the nest rather than dividing through
normal reproductive swarming.

## Brood and colony observations

**Brood** — Immature bees: eggs, larvae, and pupae.

**Open brood** — Eggs and larvae in cells that have not been capped.

**Capped brood** — Developing pupae beneath wax cappings.

**Brood pattern** — The distribution and continuity of brood on the comb. Record what
you observe rather than treating one pattern as a diagnosis by itself.

**Queen cell** — A downward-facing enlarged cell in which a queen develops. Context
helps distinguish swarm, supersedure, and emergency queen-rearing conditions.

**Temperament** — Observed colony behavior during an inspection, including calmness,
defensiveness, or flightiness. Weather and handling can affect it.

**Population strength** — An estimate of the adult bee population, commonly based on
occupied frames or seams. ApiaryLens preserves the unit or method with the value.

## Food, forage, and season

**Nectar** — Sugar-rich liquid collected from plants and processed into honey.

**Honey** — Ripened, stored food made by bees primarily from nectar.

**Pollen / bee bread** — Pollen is collected plant material; bee bread is stored and
fermented pollen used as a protein-rich food.

**Propolis** — Resinous material bees collect and use to seal and coat hive surfaces.

**Forage** — Plant resources available to bees, especially nectar, pollen, resins,
and water.

**Nectar flow / honey flow** — A period when nectar is sufficiently available for
colonies to bring in and potentially store a surplus.

**Dearth** — A period with little available nectar, often increasing robbing pressure
and the importance of monitoring stores.

**Robbing** — Bees taking stored honey from another colony or exposed equipment.

**Feeding** — Supplying sugar syrup, dry sugar, fondant, pollen substitute, or another
managed feed. Record product, amount, unit, reason, and date.

## Health, pests, and care

**Varroa / Varroa mite** — *Varroa destructor*, a parasitic mite and major honey-bee
health threat. A count is a measurement; interpretation and action thresholds depend
on method, season, location, and current guidance.

**Alcohol wash / soap wash** — A destructive sampling method used to estimate Varroa
mites on a measured sample of adult bees.

**Sugar roll** — A powdered-sugar sampling method used to dislodge and count mites.

**Sticky board** — A board placed beneath a screened bottom to observe mite fall. Its
results are not directly interchangeable with an adult-bee wash.

**Treatment** — A product or management method used to address a pest or disease.
Record dates, dosage, restrictions, and the exact label or professional guidance.

**Removal date** — The date a treatment or device must be removed. It is distinct
from a product's harvest, withdrawal, temperature, or other restrictions.

**Follow-up** — Work or an observation that should be checked later, with an optional
due date and completion state.

## ApiaryLens record language

**Inspection** — A dated observation of a hive or colony. Draft inspections can be
saved offline and completed later.

**Not synced** — The record or photo is safely stored on this device but has not yet
been accepted by the connected server.

**Synced** — The connected server accepted the current record or media version.

**Conflict** — Both local and server versions changed and require an explicit choice
or merge. ApiaryLens does not silently discard either side.

**Archived** — Hidden from normal active work without deleting its history.

**Full export** — An owner-controlled archive of records and private media for
portability and recovery. A browser's cached offline copy is not a server backup.
